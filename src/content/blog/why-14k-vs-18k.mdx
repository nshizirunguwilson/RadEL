The karat debate is mostly retail theater. Both 14k and 18k are real gold; both will outlast you. The actual differences — hardness, color, price — matter, but not in the ways you usually hear them argued. Here's the short, honest version.

## 14k is 58% gold. 18k is 75% gold.

That's it. The remaining percent is alloy — usually silver, copper, and zinc. More gold means softer metal, warmer color, and higher price per gram.

## Where the difference shows up

**Scratches and dents.** 18k picks up fine scratches more readily than 14k. For a daily-wear ring, especially one with a stone, we nudge people to 14k unless they prefer the color enough to accept the maintenance.

**Color.** 18k yellow is warmer and looks more like the gold you picture when you close your eyes. 14k yellow is slightly cooler — closer to a soft butter than a honey. Rose gold amplifies this: 18k rose is noticeably redder.

**Price.** 18k costs roughly 30% more than 14k in the same piece, accounting for the extra gold content and the care the softer metal demands.

## What we recommend

- Earrings: 18k almost always. They rarely get scratched.
- Pendants: 18k. Same reason.
- Everyday rings and bracelets: 14k, unless color is decisive.
- Stacking rings and statement pieces: whichever reads better with the stone.

There's no wrong answer. There's a version that fits how you'll actually wear it.
