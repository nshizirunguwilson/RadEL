The first thing I reach for in the morning is a loupe. Not the torch, not the files — the loupe. Before you can make anything, you have to be able to see what you're making at 10× the size your eye gives you for free. A flaw at arm's length is a disaster at 10×; a surprise at 10× is a small grace at arm's length.

## A morning, in order

I arrive at the bench at 8. The first hour is cold work: inspecting last night's cast pieces under the loupe, marking where sprues still need to be cut, tagging anything that needs a second pass. Nothing noisy yet.

At 9, the torch comes on. Soldering is the only part of the day I don't listen to anything; the torch and the metal have their own conversation and I've learned not to interrupt it. A 1.2mm ring shank joins cleanly in 4 seconds if the join is prepared right, and in 15 seconds if it isn't — I've learned to wait the extra 11 seconds rather than rush the prep.

## The one tool nobody replaces

Every bench has a favorite tool, and every jeweler has a different answer. Mine is a pair of Italian nippers from 1987 that I inherited from the jeweler who taught me. The jaws are worn in a specific way that lets them bite thin wire without crushing it. I've bought three pairs of replacements over the years; none of them cut the way these do.

## Why small runs

We cap every style at forty pieces, and most never hit forty. Scaling beyond that means hiring, which means handing off, which means the person whose hands learned the piece isn't the person making the piece anymore. At our size, that trade isn't worth it. At a different size, maybe it will be.
