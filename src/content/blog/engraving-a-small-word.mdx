A woman brought a bar pendant into the atelier last month and asked me to engrave the date of her father's wedding. It was a beautiful date — not hers, which she was clear about — and the bar was the right length. I engraved it, we both admired it, and she left happy.

Two weeks later she was back. She'd decided, on reflection, that the date wasn't what she wanted. A date, she said, "locks the piece to a moment, and I want the piece to come with me." She asked if I could buff it out and put a word there instead.

## Why dates often disappoint

Dates are precise, and precision ages badly. The ring bought for a tenth anniversary is quietly awkward at year fifteen. A date on a bar ties the piece to a moment, but the moment moves, and the piece stays.

A word doesn't move. A word that meant something to you at 28 can mean something different at 48, and the piece accommodates both.

## What to engrave instead

A single word in your native language. A verb or a noun. "Return." "Still." "Proceed." Nothing anyone else has to understand — the point of an engraving is that it's yours.

Avoid names (people move). Avoid full sentences (they look crowded at 8pt and get cut short). Avoid initials (they read as a monogram, which is a different kind of piece).

## What she chose

I buffed out the date and engraved one word: _Cadence_. I don't know what it means to her. I don't need to. The piece fits her now, and it will fit her in thirty years when the meaning has shifted a few times. That's what an engraving is for.
