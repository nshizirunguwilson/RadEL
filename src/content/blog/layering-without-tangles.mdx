Layered chains are a look and a problem. Three chains can make an outfit; three chains tangled into a nest of knots can ruin a morning. The physics of tangling is well-understood — long thin things rotating in free space always tangle — but a few rules tilt the odds.

## Rule one: skip a length

If chain A is 16", chain B should be 20" or longer. A 2" gap lets them swing without crossing planes. A 1" gap guarantees they'll braid.

## Rule two: different weights on top and bottom

Put the heavier chain on the longer layer. Heavier chains hang straighter; lighter chains drift. If you reverse it, the lighter chain climbs the heavier one and settles into its curve.

## Rule three: three is the limit

We've tried four. Four reliably tangles. If you want a four-layer look, wear three chains and a collar — the collar doesn't count as a layer because it sits above the joint.

## Rule four: clasps at the back

Always. A rotated clasp is the visible sign of a chain that's about to tangle with its neighbor. If you catch a clasp at your collarbone, stop and reset — don't power through.

## The chain pairing we reach for

A 16" Lune box chain + a 20" fine paperclip + an 24" hammered bar. Different scale, different weight, different silhouette. Layered well, you won't feel them after ten minutes.
